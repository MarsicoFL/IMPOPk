impopₖ — a hands-on tutorial

This tutorial is a short story in six chapters. You will start from a clean clone of impopₖ, spend about an hour running the four bundled examples, and by the end you will understand what each part of the toolkit does, how the pieces fit together, and how to turn it on your own data.

What you need. A laptop with Rust (rustup.rs) and Python 3.8+. No GPU. No internet after the first clone. No pangenome alignment — the examples ship precomputed inputs.

How long. ~15 min reading, ~30 min running and poking at outputs, ~15 min thinking about what it means. One hour total for the first pass.

Chapter 1 — Two cousins and a question

Picture two cousins. They share a grandmother. On some chromosomes, there is a stretch of DNA they both inherited from her, nearly letter for letter; on others they inherited entirely different stretches from their other three grandparents. If you take their genomes and slide a 10 kilobase window along them, the shared-grandmother stretch will show up as a run of windows where the two sequences are almost identical. Outside that stretch, the identity drops back to the baseline similarity any two humans have.

That is the entire observable signal this toolkit uses.

Two slightly different questions sit on top of it. If you ask "where are these two sequences identical, for whatever reason?" you are measuring identity by state (IBS). If you ask "where are they identical because they inherited the same piece from a common ancestor?" you are talking about identity by descent (IBD). IBD is a latent state you cannot observe directly; you infer it from IBS plus contiguity (real IBD segments are long, coincidental IBS matches are short and scattered). That latent-state inference is what the HMMs in this toolkit do.

Existing software that answers this question — hap-ibd, RFMix, FLARE — reads phased VCF genotypes as input. That means someone (maybe you) had to: call variants from short-read data, curate them against ascertainment bias, statistically phase the genotypes into haplotypes, and hope the phasing errors did not land at exactly the points where ancestry switches. Every step leaks information.

If you already have haplotype-resolved assemblies — and for a growing number of species, via the Human Pangenome Reference Consortium and similar projects, you do — the identity signal is already visible in the alignments. You do not need to compress it into a SNP table first.

What you gain by skipping variants. No ascertainment bias, no phasing step, structural variation is implicitly carried in the identity signal rather than discarded.

What you trade off. Coarser per-base resolution (windows are typically 10 kb), and compute scales quadratically in the number of haplotypes — so you work with panels of ~30–100 haplotypes, not thousands.

Chapter 2 — Get the tool running

Everything from here on assumes you have the repository checked out and built. It takes about a minute. 

git clone https://github.com/MarsicoFL/IMPOPk.git
cd IMPOPk
cargo build --release

If this is the first time the Rust toolchain sees the dependencies, the build takes 40–60 seconds. You should end up with five binaries in target/release/: 

ls target/release/ | grep -E '^(ibs|ibd|ibd-validate|ancestry|jacquard)$'
# ancestry
# ibd
# ibd-validate
# ibs
# jacquard

If you see anything missing, stop here and check that cargo build finished without errors. Every later step depends on these five executables existing.

Extra tools for later. Chapter 5 (running on your own pangenome) uses impg for pangenome graph queries and AGC for compressed assembly archives. You do not need them for chapters 3 and 4 — the bundled examples are precomputed.

Chapter 3 — The one input, the four decoders

Every decoder in impopₖ reads the same input: a plain TSV where each row is a pair of haplotypes, a genomic window, and their pairwise sequence identity in that window. Open data/examples/ibd/input/ibs.tsv in a text editor to see what that looks like: 

chrom           start    end      group.a  group.b  estimated.identity
CHM13#0#chr12   0        10000    SIM1#1   SIM1#2   0.998019
CHM13#0#chr12   0        10000    SIM1#1   SIM2#1   0.997927
CHM13#0#chr12   0        10000    SIM1#1   SIM2#2   0.998248
CHM13#0#chr12   0        10000    SIM1#1   SIM3#1   0.997895
...

Six columns, one row per pair-window. The chrom and start/end come from the reference coordinate system; group.a and group.b are haplotype identifiers; estimated.identity is a number between 0 and 1. That is the entire substrate the downstream tools see.

The flow from this table to a biological answer is: 

   Pangenome alignment (PAF) + assemblies (AGC)
                    │
                    │    ibs   (wraps impg similarity)
                    ▼
       Windowed pairwise identity TSV   ◄──── what the examples ship
                    │
   ┌────────────────┼──────────────────┬─────────────────┐
   ▼                ▼                  ▼                 ▼
  (awk)            ibd              ancestry          jacquard
 IBS scan       IBD segs         painted tracts      Δ coefs
                                       │
                                       ▼
                          scripts/kinship_from_ibd.py
                                kinship θ̂

You can think of the identity TSV as a kind of sufficient statistic: everything downstream reads it and does something specialized. In chapter 5 you will learn how to produce one yourself. For now, the bundled examples give you four different identity TSVs, each chosen to make one decoder easy to understand.

Chapter 4 — Running the four modes

Four mini-stories follow. Each one has the same shape: a question, a model, a command to run, a numerical answer, and a way to check the answer.

4a. IBS — where does the population share haplotypes?

The question. Across a panel of related or admixed individuals, which 10 kb windows have an unusually high fraction of near-identical haplotype pairs? These windows are candidates for recent positive selection (a favoured haplotype spread and is now over-represented), bottleneck events, or shared founder effects.

The model. No HMM. For each window we simply compute

E(w) = #{ pairs (a,b) with S(a,b;w) ≥ θ } / #{ pairs }

with θ = 0.9999 by default. This is the raw fuel for selection scans.

The command.

cd data/examples/ibs
bash run.sh

What you are running. A short awk one-liner applied to a precomputed identity TSV covering a 1 Mb slice of chr12 for 6 HPRC European haplotypes. No pangenome query, no HMM, no training — it is pure aggregation.

The answer.

head -5 output/ibs_enrichment.tsv
# chrom          start     end        n_pairs  n_ibs  frac_ibs
# CHM13#0#chr12  15000000  15009999   15       1      0.066667
# CHM13#0#chr12  15010000  15019999   15       3      0.200000
# CHM13#0#chr12  15020000  15029999   15       3      0.200000
# CHM13#0#chr12  15030000  15039999   15       3      0.200000
# ...

Sort by frac_ibs descending and the windows with the most within-population sharing bubble up. On a full-chromosome scan, the top windows coincide with well-known selection targets — for example, the lactase-persistence region on chromosome 2 around LCT/MCM6. This bundled example is only 1 Mb of a neutral chr12 region, so the absolute numbers are unremarkable on purpose: you are meant to see the mechanics, not discover biology.

Check yourself. Before moving on, answer two questions in your head: (i) why do all windows have exactly 15 pairs? (ii) if a population had a recent selective sweep at one window, what would you expect frac_ibs to do there?

4b. IBD — which specific segments are inherited together?

The question. Given two haplotypes, find every contiguous chromosomal stretch they inherited from a common ancestor. Not just "are they related" — where they share DNA and how long each piece is.

The model. A two-state hidden Markov model. The observation at window w for a pair (a, b) is the identity S(a, b; w). The hidden state is IBD or non-IBD.

  • Emissions are Gaussians, one per state. The IBD state has a mean close to 1 and a small variance; the non-IBD state has a lower mean and wider variance. The tool initialises these with k-means on the observed identity distribution, then refines them by Baum-Welch expectation-maximisation.
  • Transitions encode a small prior probability of entering an IBD state (--p-enter-ibd, default 10⁻⁴) and a segment-length prior matching the expected coalescent recombination scale.
  • Decoding uses the Viterbi path for the maximum-likelihood assignment, plus a forward-backward pass for per-window posteriors. Short segments below --min-len-bp are discarded.

The command.

cd data/examples/ibd
bash run.sh

What you are running. The identity TSV in this example is synthetic — regenerable via scripts/generate_synthetic_ibd_example.py — because unrelated HPRC individuals have no real IBD and an empty output would look broken. The fabricated data has two known IBD segments:

  • SIM1#1 × SIM2#1 between 1.25 Mb and 3.75 Mb (2.5 Mb long)
  • SIM1#2 × SIM3#1 between 0.50 Mb and 2.00 Mb (1.5 Mb long)

The answer.

cat output/ibd.tsv
# chrom  start    end      group.a  group.b  n_windows  mean_identity  mean_posterior  min_posterior  max_posterior  lod_score
# chr12  1250000  3750000  SIM1#1   SIM2#1   250        0.999941       1.0000          1.0000          1.0000         2280.21
# chr12   500000  2000000  SIM1#2   SIM3#1   150        0.999948       1.0000          1.0000          1.0000         1446.78

Both known segments are recovered at their exact boundaries, with posterior probability 1.0 at every window and log-odds scores above a thousand. If you want to sanity-check that the tool did not just memorise, edit scripts/generate_synthetic_ibd_example.py to put a segment somewhere else and rerun — the decoder will track you.

How to read the output columns. n_windows is the segment length in 10 kb windows. mean_identity is the observed S averaged across the segment. mean_posterior is how confident the HMM is, averaged across the segment. lod_score is the log-odds of IBD versus non-IBD; in practice, any value above ~5 is overwhelming evidence.

4c. Ancestry — which population does each piece come from?

The question. Given a query haplotype from an admixed individual, assign each 10 kb window to one of K reference populations (AFR, EUR, EAS, …).

The model. A K-state HMM. For each window, for each candidate population k, we compute

fk(w) = max { S(q, h; w) : h ∈ reference haplotypes of population k }

— the best-case similarity of the query haplotype q to any reference haplotype in that population. Those per-population scores go through a softmax to become emission probabilities:

log P(ow | sw=k) = fk(w) / τ − log Σj exp(fj(w) / τ)

Here τ is a softmax temperature. In the default mode (--estimate-params, used in this example) a single scalar τ is fit from the identity distribution across windows. An optional flag --pairwise-emissions switches to a per-pair adaptive τ estimated via a Bradley-Terry pairwise-contrast model, which lets the HMM treat closely-related population pairs differently from well-separated ones. Transitions use a small switch probability (--switch-prob).

The command.

cd data/examples/ancestry
bash run.sh

The inputs.

  • input/ibs.tsv — windowed identities across a 330 kb slice of chr12 (33 × 10 kb windows) for a 16-haplotype panel of AFR + EUR + AMR HPRC individuals.
  • input/populations.tsv — two columns mapping each reference haplotype to its population label.
  • input/queries.txt — the haplotypes you want painted.

The answer.

head -5 output/ancestry.tsv
# chrom  start     end        sample     ancestry  n_windows  mean_similarity  mean_posterior  discriminability  lod_score
# chr12  15000000  15329999   HG00639#2  EUR       33         0.999906         0.999207        0.000667          2.1735
# chr12  15000000  15329999   HG00639#1  EUR       33         0.999915         0.601676        0.000285          0.0530
# ...

Both query haplotypes of HG00639 (a CEU individual) are painted EUR across the 330 kb slice — which is the right call for this non-admixed region. Notice the asymmetry in mean_posterior: one haplotype is called with high confidence (0.999), the other barely scraping through (0.60). Why? Because at 16 haplotypes across 330 kb the panel is tiny; discriminability (the gap between the top-two state probabilities) is only ~0.0003 at most windows, which means several populations all look almost equally good.

This is a toy example. 16 haps × 330 kb is deliberately small so the tutorial stays fast. On realistic inputs — a 46-haplotype panel across a full 133 Mb chromosome — the same HMM reaches 95–97% concordance against a ground-truth simulation. You can reproduce those runs with the pipeline in Chapter 5.
How to read the new columns. mean_similarity is the average best-match identity to the called population. discriminability is the per-window gap between the top two population probabilities — high means a clear call, low means the window could plausibly belong to more than one population. lod_score is log-odds of the called state vs the second-best alternative.

4d. Founder painting — same tool, individuals as states

The same ancestry HMM works unchanged if each "state" is an individual haplotype instead of a population. That is the standard pedigree application: given the four grandparent haplotypes and one grandchild haplotype, which grandmother or grandfather did each 10 kb stretch come from?

The command.

cd data/examples/pedigree
bash run.sh

What you are running. The identity TSV is again synthetic (scripts/generate_synthetic_pedigree_example.py) because unrelated HPRC individuals do not form a pedigree. The fabricated descendant CHILD is a four-block mosaic of founders GP1–GP4, equally spaced:

RegionTrue founder
0 – 1.25 MbGP1
1.25 – 2.50 MbGP2
2.50 – 3.75 MbGP3
3.75 – 5.00 MbGP4

The answer.

cat output/painting.tsv
# chrom              start     end       sample  ancestry  n_windows  mean_similarity  ...
# CHM13#0#chr12      0         1250000   CHILD   GP1       125        0.999939         ...
# CHM13#0#chr12      1250000   2500000   CHILD   GP2       125        0.999944         ...
# CHM13#0#chr12      2500000   3750000   CHILD   GP3       125        0.999947         ...
# CHM13#0#chr12      3750000   5000000   CHILD   GP4       125        0.999947         ...

Four tracts, at the exact boundaries, each assigned to the correct founder, posteriors above 0.99. Drop in a real three-generation pedigree and the same run produces an authentic founder painting — the CEPH 1463 Platinum Pedigree is the canonical validation set; its inheritance vectors give per-segment ground truth.

4e. Kinship — how related are they overall?

Once you have IBD segments between two diploid individuals, the kinship coefficient θ̂ falls out as a simple sum. For a diploid pair (A, B) with haplotypes (A₁, A₂) and (B₁, B₂):

θ̂ = Σα,β ∈ {1,2} LIBD(Aα, Bβ) / (4 · L)

where LIBD(Aα, Bβ) is the total detected IBD length between the α-th haplotype of A and the β-th haplotype of B, and L is the chromosome length. For reference, full siblings should cluster near θ̂ = 0.25, half-siblings and grandparent relationships near 0.125, first cousins near 0.0625.

The post-processor scripts/kinship_from_ibd.py takes the ibd.tsv from the previous mode and turns it into a per-pair kinship table:

python3 scripts/kinship_from_ibd.py \
    --ibd          data/examples/ibd/expected_output/ibd.tsv \
    --chrom-length 5000000
# individual_a  individual_b  total_ibd_bp  theta_hat
# SIM1          SIM2          2500000       0.125000
# SIM1          SIM3          1500000       0.075000

The pairs picked up from the synthetic IBD output produce θ̂ of 0.125 and 0.075 — the first pair looks like a grandparent-grandchild relationship, the second a first cousin. This is only illustrative of the arithmetic; for real relatedness inference you want more than one chromosome.

The Jacquard Δ variant

If you need the full nine-state identity decomposition (useful when you cannot assume the individuals are outbred, or you want to detect inbreeding), run jacquard instead. It takes the IBS table and the four haplotype IDs of the pair and prints the nine Δ coefficients to stdout: 

target/release/jacquard \
    --ibs    data/examples/ibd/input/ibs.tsv \
    --hap-a1 SIM1#1  --hap-a2 SIM1#2 \
    --hap-b1 SIM2#1  --hap-b2 SIM2#2

The nine deltas sum to one and give a richer picture than the scalar θ̂. For most biobank-style analyses the scalar θ̂ is what you want; reach for jacquard when the identity-state decomposition matters.

Chapter 5 — Your own data

You have reached the point where the identity TSV feels familiar. Chapter 3 took it as given; this chapter shows how to produce one from scratch.

What you need

  • A PAF alignment of the panel you care about. For human work, HPRCv2 distributes PAFs against CHM13 via the HPRC data portal. For non-human projects you can align haplotype-resolved assemblies yourself with minimap2 (-x asm5 for intra-species) or build a full pangenome graph with PGGB.
  • An AGC archive of the same assemblies — see refresh-bio/agc.
  • impg installed (cargo install impg).

Step 1. Define your panel

A panel_subset.txt with one haplotype or sample ID per line:

HG00097#1
HG00097#2
HG01884#1
HG01884#2
NA19017#1
NA19017#2

A populations.tsv (tab-separated, two columns):

EUR    HG00097#1
EUR    HG00097#2
AFR    HG01884#1
AFR    HG01884#2
AMR    NA19017#1
AMR    NA19017#2

A queries.txt listing which haplotypes you want painted:

NA19017#1
NA19017#2

Step 2. Compute windowed pairwise identity

ibs -a                      data/alignments/hprc_chr12.paf.gz \
    --sequence-files        data/assemblies/HPRC_r2.agc \
    -r                      CHM13 \
    --region                chr12:1-133324548 \
    --size                  10000 \
    --subset-sequence-list  panel_subset.txt \
    --threads               8 \
    --output                ibs_chr12.tsv

This drives impg similarity over every 10 kb window in the requested region, filters, canonicalises, and streams the result. As a rough order of magnitude: a full 133 Mb chromosome with 46 haplotypes on 8 threads takes about 8 minutes. Memory is the dominant cost.

Step 3. Feed the TSV to the decoders you want

# IBD
ibd --similarity-file ibs_chr12.tsv \
    --region chr12:1-133324548 --region-length 133324548 \
    --size 10000 --min-len-bp 2000000 \
    --population Generic \
    --threads 8 --output ibd_chr12.tsv

# Local ancestry
ancestry --similarity-file ibs_chr12.tsv \
         --window-size 10000 \
         --populations  populations.tsv \
         --query-samples queries.txt \
         --emission-model max --estimate-params \
         --threads 8 --output ancestry_chr12.tsv

# Kinship
python3 scripts/kinship_from_ibd.py \
        --ibd ibd_chr12.tsv \
        --chrom-length 133324548

That is the full pipeline. The rest of your work is panel curation and interpretation, not plumbing.

Chapter 6 — When things go sideways

SymptomLikely causeWhat to do
ibd reports 0 segments. The panel has no true IBD, or all segments are shorter than --min-len-bp. Lower --min-len-bp to 500 kb for exploratory runs. Verify that the panel actually contains related individuals.
ancestry reports high discriminability but low mean_posterior. Panel too small to separate close populations. Expand the reference panel, or merge populations at the continental level instead of sub-continental.
WARNING: >30% of windows are uncertain. Identity contrast between populations is near the noise floor. Increase window size (--window-size 25000) or move to a continental-level contrast.
jacquard: "haplotype not found". The haplotype ID in --hap-a1 etc. does not match the IBS TSV group.a/group.b format. Use the short form (e.g. HG00097#1). impopₖ strips anything after the second # when matching.
Out of memory on a real pangenome. Using the full HPRCv2 panel (~290 sequences) blows past 49 GB per window with default impg behaviour. Use a reduced panel of ~40 haplotypes (roughly 5 per population × 8 populations). Most continental signals are recoverable at this scale.
